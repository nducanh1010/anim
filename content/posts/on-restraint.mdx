The first instinct, when given a blank canvas, is to fill it. A second
accent color, because one is lonely. A drop shadow, because the card
feels naked. A gradient, because flat is the enemy. We have all done
this. The work is doing it less.

Restraint is not a synonym for minimal. Minimal is a count: fewer
elements, more whitespace, no decoration. Restraint is a posture. It
asks, of every element, "do you earn this space?" The element that
cannot answer is the element that goes.

Three rules I keep returning to:

**One accent, used rarely.** A single saturated color earns its meaning
by being absent most of the time. The moment it appears on a button, a
link, a focus ring, it carries information. The moment it is also on a
border, a heading, a divider, it is wallpaper.

**Tinted neutrals, never pure gray.** A neutral that has zero chroma
feels lifeless next to a brand color. Adding a 0.01 chroma tint toward
the brand hue is invisible in isolation and powerful in aggregate. The
surface feels like it was made by the same hand as the type that sits
on it.

**Type does the work.** Hierarchy through weight and size contrast is
stronger than hierarchy through color. A 700-weight headline on a
neutral background reads as confident. The same headline in brand
color reads as a teenager. Reach for the type weight first.

The point of restraint is not to make things boring. The point is to
make the things that matter louder. Restraint is generosity toward
the reader: you are not making them wade through your enthusiasm to
find the sentence they came for.
